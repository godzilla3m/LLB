## Q1. What do you understand by a Company? Differentiate between Company and Partnership firm?

Here‚Äôs a clear and exam‚Äëstyle answer written in simple sentences, with relatable Indian names and places as *example*s.

---

**Meaning of a Company**

A **company** is a legal entity created under law. It is formed by a group of people who come together to carry on business. A company has a separate legal personality. This means it is different from the people who own it. It can own property, enter into contracts, and sue or be sued in its own name.

> For *example*, if **Prateek**, **Krishna**, and **Kanishth** register a company in Lucknow, the company itself becomes a separate person in the eyes of law. Even if Prateek leaves, the company continues to exist.

A company is usually formed under the **Companies Act, 2013** in India. It can be a private company or a public company.

**Key Features of a Company**

* **Separate legal entity**: The company is different from its members.  
* **Limited liability**: Members are liable only up to the amount they invested.  
* **Perpetual succession**: The company continues even if members change or die.  
* **Transferability of shares**: In public companies, shares can be transferred easily.  
* **Regulation by law**: Companies must follow strict rules under the Companies Act.

---

**Meaning of a Partnership Firm**

A **partnership firm** is formed when two or more people agree to carry on business together and share profits. It is governed by the **Indian Partnership Act, 1932**.

Unlike a company, a partnership firm does not have a separate legal personality. The partners themselves are the business.

For *example*, if **Raghavendra**, **Laukendra**, and **Saurabh** start a cloth shop in Varanasi as partners, the firm is not separate from them. If the firm takes a loan, the partners are personally responsible for repayment.

**Key Features of a Partnership Firm**

* **No separate legal entity**: The firm and partners are the same in law.  
* **Unlimited liability**: Partners are personally responsible for debts.  
* **Mutual agency**: Each partner can bind the firm by his actions.  
* **Limited life**: If a partner dies or leaves, the firm usually dissolves.  
* **Less regulation**: Partnership firms are easier to form and run compared to companies.

---

**Difference Between Company and Partnership Firm**

| Basis | Company | Partnership Firm |
| ----- | ----- | ----- |
| **Legal status** | Separate legal entity | Not separate from partners |
| **Liability** | Limited liability of members | Unlimited liability of partners |
| **Formation** | Registered under Companies Act | Formed by agreement under Partnership Act |
| **Continuity** | Perpetual succession | Ends if partner dies or leaves |
| **Ownership transfer** | Shares can be transferred easily | Transfer needs consent of all partners |
| **Management** | Managed by directors | Managed directly by partners |
| **Regulation** | Strict legal compliance | Less legal formalities |

---

**Simple *Example*s for Better Understanding**

* Suppose **Satyam** and **Sachkeerat** start a partnership firm in Patna to sell books. If the firm suffers a loss, both are personally responsible. Their personal property can be used to pay debts.  
* On the other hand, if **Prateek**, **Krishna**, and **Kanishth** start a company in Delhi to run a software business, their liability is limited. If the company suffers a loss, only the company‚Äôs assets are used. Their personal houses or cars are safe.

---

**Conclusion**

A company and a partnership firm are two different forms of business organization. A company is a separate legal entity with limited liability and perpetual succession. A partnership firm is not separate from its partners, and partners have unlimited liability.

In simple words, a company is safer for members because their personal property is protected, but it requires more legal compliance. A partnership firm is easier to start and manage, but partners carry higher risk.

Thus, the choice between a company and a partnership depends on the nature of business, the number of people involved, and the level of risk they are willing to take.

## Q2. What do you mean by doctrine of 'ultra-vires'? Discuss the consequences of ultra-vires acts of the company.

Here‚Äôs a clear and exam‚Äëstyle answer written in simple sentences, with relatable Indian names and places as *example*s.

---

**Doctrine of Ultra Vires (‡§∂‡§ï‡•ç‡§§‡§ø‡§Ø‡•ã‡§Ç ‡§∏‡•á ‡§™‡§∞‡•á)**

The word *ultra vires* is a Latin term. It means "beyond powers / ‡§∂‡§ï‡•ç‡§§‡§ø‡§Ø‡•ã‡§Ç ‡§∏‡•á ‡§™‡§∞‡•á." In company law, the doctrine of ultra vires says that a company cannot do anything beyond the powers given to it by its Memorandum of Association. The Memorandum of Association is the basic document of a company. It defines the objectives and scope of the company.

If a company does something outside its objects, that act is called ultra vires. Such an act is void (invalid) and cannot be ratified even if all shareholders agree.

For *example*, if **Prateek**, **Krishna**, and **Kanishth** form a company in Delhi to run a textile business, the company can only do activities related to textiles. If the company suddenly starts a hotel business in Jaipur, that act is ultra vires because it is beyond the objects written in the Memorandum.

---

**Purpose of the Doctrine**

The doctrine protects shareholders and creditors. It ensures that the company uses its funds only for the purposes for which it was created. It prevents misuse of money and protects outsiders dealing with the company.

---

**Consequences of Ultra Vires Acts**

1. **Void nature of the act**  
    Ultra vires acts are void. They cannot be enforced in a court of law. Even if all members agree, the act cannot be made valid.  
    *Example*: If **Raghavendra** and **Laukendra** are directors of a company in Varanasi and they invest company money in a cinema hall, but the Memorandum allows only trading in electronics, the investment is void.  
2. **Injunction by shareholders**  
    Shareholders can stop the company from doing ultra vires acts by filing an injunction in court.  
    *Example*: If **Saurabh** and **Satyam**, who are shareholders in a textile company in Patna, see that directors are planning to use funds for a hotel business outside the objects, they can approach the court to stop it.  
3. **Personal liability of directors**  
    Directors may be personally liable if they authorize ultra vires acts. They are supposed to act within the powers of the company.  
    *Example*: If **Sachkeerat** is a director of a company in Lucknow and he signs a contract for mining business when the company is only allowed to run a school, he may be personally responsible for losses.  
4. **Company‚Äôs property protection**  
   If company property is obtained through an ultra vires act (an act beyond the company‚Äôs objects), the company has the right to recover that property.  
   **Simple *Example*:**  
   **Prateek** and **Krishna** use the company‚Äôs money to buy land in Kanpur for farming. Farming is not mentioned in the company‚Äôs objects. This act is ultra vires.  
   Therefore, the company can take back that land, because the money used belonged to the company and was misused.  
5. **Contracts are unenforceable**  
    Any contract made under ultra vires is unenforceable. Outsiders cannot enforce such contracts against the company.  
    *Example*: If **Kanishth** signs a contract with a supplier in Bhopal for a hotel project, but the company‚Äôs object is only textile trading, the supplier cannot enforce the contract against the company.  
6. **Protection of creditors**  
    Creditors are protected because the company cannot use funds for purposes outside its objects. This ensures that money is not wasted.  
    *Example*: If **Raghavendra** lends money to a company in Allahabad for textile trading, he is safe because the company cannot use that money for unrelated businesses like cinema halls.

*Here‚Äôs a simple list of ***example*s of creditors**:*

* ***Banks** (like State Bank of India, Punjab National Bank)*  
* ***Suppliers** (who provide goods on credit)*  
* ***Friends or relatives** (who lend money personally)*  
* ***Service providers** (like transport companies, electricity boards)*  
* ***Government** (for unpaid taxes such as GST or income tax)*  
* ***Employees** (for unpaid salaries or wages)*

---

**Simple Illustration**

Suppose a company is formed by **Prateek**, **Krishna**, and **Kanishth** in Delhi to manufacture clothes. The Memorandum clearly states "textile manufacturing." Later, directors **Raghavendra** and **Laukendra** decide to open a hotel in Jaipur with company funds. This act is ultra vires. Shareholders like **Saurabh** and **Satyam** can stop it. If **Sachkeerat** signs a contract with a hotel supplier, the contract is void. The company cannot be forced to pay.

---

**Conclusion**

The doctrine of ultra vires is an important principle in company law. It ensures that a company acts only within the powers given by its Memorandum of Association. Ultra vires acts are void and cannot be ratified. They may lead to personal liability of directors, injunctions by shareholders, and unenforceable contracts.

In simple words, the doctrine protects shareholders, creditors, and the public from misuse of company funds. It keeps the company within its legal boundaries. For students and business people, the lesson is clear: always check the objects of the company before making decisions.

## Q3. Explain the meaning and importance of 'Prospectus'. Discuss the liability of persons issuing a prospectus containing false statements.

Here‚Äôs a clear and exam‚Äëstyle answer in simple sentences, with relatable names and Indian places as *example*s.

---

**Meaning of Prospectus**

A prospectus is a legal document issued by a company when it wants to invite the public to buy its shares or debentures. It contains details about the company, its objectives, financial position, and the terms of issue of shares.

**In simple words**, a prospectus is like an advertisement but with legal value. It tells the public why they should invest in the company and what benefits they may get.

For *example*, if **Prateek**, **Krishna**, and **Kanishth** form a company in Delhi and want to raise money from the public, they must issue a prospectus. The prospectus will explain the company‚Äôs textile business, its profits, and how many shares are available.

---

**Importance of Prospectus**

1. **Information to investors**  
    A prospectus gives important information to investors so they can decide whether to invest.  
    *Example*: If **Raghavendra** in Varanasi wants to buy shares of a company, he will read the prospectus to understand the business.  
2. **Transparency**  
    It ensures transparency because the company must disclose all material facts.  
    *Example*: If **Laukendra** is thinking of investing in a company in Lucknow, he can trust the prospectus to give him clear details.  
3. **Legal safeguard**  
    The prospectus protects investors because false statements can lead to liability.  
    *Example*: If **Saurabh** invests in a company in Patna based on a false prospectus, he can take legal action against the company.  
4. **Public confidence**  
    A prospectus builds confidence among the public. People are more willing to invest when they see official documents.  
    *Example*: If **Satyam** and **Sachkeerat** see a prospectus of a company in Allahabad, they may feel secure in investing.

---

**Liability for False Statements in Prospectus**

If a prospectus contains false statements, the persons who issued it can be held liable. Liability can be civil or criminal.

1. **Civil liability**

   * If an investor suffers loss because of false statements, he can claim **compensation**.  
   * Directors, promoters, and others who authorized the issue of the prospectus are responsible.  
      *Example*: If **Prateek** and **Krishna** issue a prospectus in Delhi saying the company made huge profits, but in reality it made losses, investors like **Kanishth** can **sue them** for compensation.  
2. **Criminal liability**

   * If false statements are made knowingly, it is a criminal offence.  
   * The persons responsible may face fines or imprisonment.  
      *Example*: If **Raghavendra** and **Laukendra** deliberately hide debts of the company in Varanasi and issue a prospectus showing only profits, they can be punished under law.  
3. **Liability of directors and promoters**

   * Directors and promoters must ensure that the prospectus is truthful.  
   * If they fail, they are **personally liable**.  
      *Example*: If **Saurabh** and **Satyam** are directors of a company in Patna and allow a false prospectus to be issued, they are personally responsible.  
4. **Investor‚Äôs right to rescind (cancel) contract**

   * If an investor buys shares based on false statements, he can **cancel** the contract.  
      *Example*: If **Sachkeerat** buys shares of a company in Lucknow after reading a false prospectus, he can return the shares and get his money back.

---

**Simple Illustration**

Suppose a company is formed by **Prateek**, **Krishna**, and **Kanishth** in Delhi to run a textile business. They issue a prospectus claiming that the company has earned ‚Çπ10 crore profit. In reality, the company has suffered losses. Investors like **Raghavendra**, **Laukendra**, **Saurabh**, **Satyam**, and **Sachkeerat** buy shares based on this false information. Later, when the truth comes out, the investors can sue the directors for compensation. The directors may also face criminal charges for cheating.

---

**Conclusion**

A prospectus is an important document in company law. It informs the public about the company and helps them decide whether to invest. It ensures transparency and builds trust. However, if a prospectus contains false statements, the persons who issued it are liable both civilly and criminally.

In simple words, the prospectus is the foundation of public investment. Honesty in the prospectus protects investors and strengthens the company. Falsehood in the prospectus harms investors and brings punishment to those responsible.

## Q4. "Majority will have its way but minority must be allowed to have it say." Discuss the concept of prevention of opperssion and mismanagement in context of relevant cases ?
Here‚Äôs a clear and exam‚Äëstyle answer in simple sentences, with relatable names and Indian places as *example*s.

---

**Meaning of the Principle**

The phrase "Majority will have its way but minority must be allowed to have its say" reflects the balance in company law. In a company, decisions are usually taken by majority shareholders. However, the law also protects minority shareholders from oppression and mismanagement.

Oppression means unfair treatment of minority shareholders by the majority. Mismanagement means running the company in a way that harms shareholders or the public interest.

For *example*, if **Prateek**, **Krishna**, and **Kanishth** hold majority shares in a company in Delhi, they can decide policies. But if they **misuse** their power to harm minority shareholders like **Raghavendra** and **Laukendra**, the law steps in to protect the minority.

*Example* of **misuse**: pass a resolution to issue new shares only to themselves at a very low price, thereby reducing the ownership percentage of minority shareholders like Raghavendra and Laukendra

---

**Concept of Prevention of Oppression and Mismanagement**

The Companies Act, 2013 in India provides remedies to minority shareholders. **Sections 241 to 246** deal with oppression and mismanagement. Minority shareholders can approach the National Company Law Tribunal (**NCLT**) if they feel oppressed or if the company is mismanaged.

The aim is to ensure **fairness**. Majority shareholders cannot misuse their power to harm minority interests.

---

***Example*s of Oppression**

1. **Unfair removal of minority shareholders**  
    *Example*: If **Saurabh** and **Satyam** are minority shareholders in a company in Patna, and majority shareholders force them out without reason, it is oppression.  
2. **Refusal to declare dividends**  
    *Example*: If **Sachkeerat** holds minority shares in a company in Lucknow, and majority shareholders refuse to declare dividends even when profits exist, it is oppression.  
3. **Issue of shares to dilute minority**  
    *Example*: If **Prateek** and **Krishna** issue new shares in Delhi only to themselves to reduce **Kanishth‚Äôs** shareholding, it is oppression.

**Tip:** minority shareholders are the owners of fewer shares in a company. They may still attend meetings, vote, and receive dividends, but they cannot control the company‚Äôs policies alone.

---

***Example*s of Mismanagement**

1. **Misuse of company funds**  
    *Example*: If **Raghavendra** and **Laukendra** use company money in Varanasi for personal purposes, it is mismanagement.  
2. **Failure to maintain proper accounts**  
    *Example*: If directors like **Saurabh** and **Satyam** in Patna do not keep records, creditors and shareholders suffer.  
3. **Risky investments without approval**  
    *Example*: If **Sachkeerat** invests company money in Lucknow in a gambling business, it is mismanagement.

---

**Relevant Cases**

1. **Shanti Prasad Jain v. Kalinga Tubes Ltd. (1965)**  
    In this case, minority shareholders alleged oppression. The Supreme Court held that oppression must be continuous and burdensome. Occasional acts are not enough.  
2. **Needle Industries (India) Ltd. v. Needle Industries Newey (India) Holding Ltd. (1981)**  
    In this case, issue of shares to dilute minority was challenged. The Court protected minority shareholders and emphasized fairness.  
3. **Sangramsinh P. Gaekwad v. Shantadevi P. Gaekwad (2005)**  
    The Court explained that not every dispute is oppression. Only acts that are harsh and unfair to minority count.

---

**Remedies Available**

* Minority shareholders can apply to NCLT under Section 241\.  
* NCLT can order changes in management.  
* NCLT can cancel unfair decisions.  
* NCLT can regulate conduct of company affairs.  
* In extreme cases, NCLT can order winding up of the company.

---

**Simple Illustration**

Suppose a company is formed in Delhi by **Prateek**, **Krishna**, and **Kanishth**. Minority shareholders are **Raghavendra**, **Laukendra**, **Saurabh**, **Satyam**, and **Sachkeerat**. The majority decides not to declare dividends even though profits exist. They also issue new shares only to themselves to reduce minority power. This is oppression. If they also misuse company funds for personal travel to Jaipur, it is mismanagement. Minority shareholders can approach NCLT for relief.

---

**Conclusion**

The principle "Majority will have its way but minority must be allowed to have its say" ensures balance in company law. Majority shareholders can control decisions, but they cannot misuse power. Minority shareholders are protected against oppression and mismanagement through legal remedies.

In simple words, company law gives majority the right to rule but ensures minority is not crushed. This balance maintains fairness, protects investors, and strengthens trust in corporate governance.

## Q5. What do you mean by borrowing power of the company? What remedies are open to a lender, if a company resort to ultra-vires borrowing?

Here‚Äôs a clear and exam‚Äëstyle answer in simple sentences, with relatable names and Indian places as *example*s.

---

**Borrowing Power of the Company**

Borrowing power means the authority of a company to raise money by taking loans or issuing debentures. A company may need funds for expansion, working capital, or new projects. The power to borrow is given by the Memorandum of Association and Articles of Association.

*(**Debenture** means a type of loan taken by a company from the public.)*

In simple words, borrowing power allows a company to take money from outsiders with a promise to repay.

For *example*, if **Prateek**, **Krishna**, and **Kanishth** form a textile company in Delhi, they may borrow money from a bank in Lucknow to buy new machines. This borrowing is valid if it is within the company‚Äôs powers.

---

**Types of Borrowing**

1. **Borrowing with security**  
    The company gives security like land, building, or machinery.  
    *Example*: If **Raghavendra** lends money to a company in Varanasi and takes the company‚Äôs warehouse as security, it is secured borrowing.  
2. **Borrowing without security**  
    The company borrows without giving any property as security.  
    *Example*: If **Laukendra** lends ‚Çπ10 lakh to a company in Patna without any security, it is unsecured borrowing.  
3. **Borrowing by issue of debentures**  
    The company issues debentures to raise funds.  
    *Example*: If **Saurabh** buys debentures of a company in Allahabad, he becomes a creditor.

---

**Ultra Vires Borrowing**

Ultra vires borrowing means borrowing beyond the powers of the company. If the Memorandum does not allow borrowing, or if borrowing is beyond the limit, it is ultra vires. Such borrowing is void and cannot be enforced against the company.

For *example*, if **Satyam** and **Sachkeerat** are directors of a company in Lucknow and they borrow money for a hotel business, but the company‚Äôs object is only textile trading, the borrowing is ultra vires.

---

**Remedies Open to a Lender in Case of Ultra Vires Borrowing**

1. **Injunction to stop borrowing**  
    A lender can stop the company from using borrowed money for ultra vires purposes.  
    *Example*: If **Prateek** lends money to a company in Delhi and sees that directors are using it for gambling, he can approach the court for injunction.  
2. **Right against directors**  
    Directors may be personally liable if they authorize ultra vires borrowing.  
    *Example*: If **Krishna** and **Kanishth** borrow money in Jaipur for purposes outside the Memorandum, the lender can sue them personally.  
3. **Tracing of money**  
    If the borrowed money is still with the company, the lender can recover it.  
    *Example*: If **Raghavendra** lends money to a company in Varanasi and the money is unused in the company‚Äôs bank account, he can claim it back.  
4. **Recovery of property**  
    If the borrowed money is used to buy property, the lender can claim rights over that property.  
    *Example*: If **Laukendra‚Äôs** loan is used to buy land in Patna, he can recover his money by claiming interest in that land.  
5. **Restitution (return) under equity**  
    If the company has benefited from the money, the lender can demand restitution.  
    *Example*: If **Saurabh** lends money to a company in Allahabad and the company uses it to pay off debts, he can claim repayment (return / refund) under equity.  
6. **No right against company for ultra vires borrowing**  
    The lender cannot enforce the loan against the company because the act is void.  
    *Example*: If **Satyam** lends money to a company in Lucknow for hotel business outside its objects, he cannot sue the company directly.

---

**Simple Illustration**

Suppose a company in Delhi is formed by **Prateek**, **Krishna**, and **Kanishth** for textile trading. They borrow ‚Çπ50 lakh from **Raghavendra** in Varanasi to start a cinema hall. This borrowing is ultra vires because the company‚Äôs object is textile trading. **Raghavendra** cannot enforce the loan against the company. However, he can sue the directors personally, recover money if it is unused, or claim rights over property purchased with his money.

---

**Conclusion**

Borrowing power of a company means its authority to raise funds through loans or debentures. It is valid only if allowed by the Memorandum and Articles. Ultra vires borrowing is void and cannot bind the company. However, the lender has remedies like injunction, suing directors personally, tracing money, or claiming property.

**In simple words,** borrowing power is necessary for business growth, but it must stay within legal limits. Ultra vires borrowing protects shareholders and creditors, while remedies ensure that lenders are not left helpless.

## Q6. What do you mean by floating charge? How does it differ from a fixed charge? When does a floating charge become fixed charge?

Here‚Äôs a clear and exam‚Äëstyle answer in simple sentences, with relatable names and Indian places as *example*s.

---

**Meaning of Floating Charge**

A floating charge is a type of security given by a company to a lender over its assets. It is called "floating" because it is not attached to any specific asset at the time of creation. Instead, it covers a class of assets that keeps changing in the ordinary course of business, such as stock, raw materials, or receivables.

In simple words, a floating charge moves with the assets. The company can use, sell, or replace those assets until the charge becomes fixed.

For *example*, if **Prateek**, **Krishna**, and **Kanishth** run a textile company in Delhi and borrow money from a bank in Lucknow, they may give a floating charge over their stock of cloth. The stock keeps changing every day, but the charge continues to cover whatever stock exists.

---

**Meaning of Fixed Charge**

A fixed charge is a security that is attached to a specific asset of the company. The company cannot sell or dispose of that asset without the lender‚Äôs permission.

In simple words, a fixed charge is tied to one particular property.

For *example*, if **Raghavendra** lends money to a company in Varanasi and takes the company‚Äôs factory building as security, that is a fixed charge. The company cannot sell the building without his consent.

---

**Difference Between Floating Charge and Fixed Charge**

1. **Nature of asset**

   * Floating charge covers assets that change, like stock or receivables.  
   * Fixed charge covers specific assets like land, building, or machinery.  
2. **Control over asset**

   * Under floating charge, the company can freely use or sell the assets until the charge crystallizes.  
   * Under fixed charge, the company cannot deal with the asset without lender‚Äôs permission.  
3. **Flexibility**

   * Floating charge gives flexibility to the company to run its business smoothly.  
   * Fixed charge restricts the company because the asset is locked.  
4. **Security for lender**

   * Floating charge is weaker security because assets keep changing.  
   * Fixed charge is stronger security because it is tied to a permanent asset.

***Example* for clarity:**

* If **Laukendra** lends money to a company in Patna and takes a floating charge over its stock of books, the company can keep selling books in the market.  
* If **Saurabh** lends money to a company in Allahabad and takes a fixed charge over its warehouse, the company cannot sell the warehouse without his approval.

---

**When Does a Floating Charge Become a Fixed Charge?**

A floating charge becomes fixed when it "crystallizes i.e. becomes solid or fixed." Crystallization means the charge attaches to specific assets. This happens in certain situations:

1. **Company goes into liquidation**  
    *Example*: If **Satyam‚Äôs** textile company in Lucknow is wound up, the floating charge over its stock becomes fixed on whatever stock exists at that time. Meaning, the **leftover cloths in the warehouse become the fixed charge**. The lender can claim rights over those cloths to recover their money.  
2. **Company stops business**  
    *Example*: If **Sachkeerat‚Äôs** company in Kanpur stops trading, the floating charge over its raw materials becomes fixed on whatever raw materials remain.  
3. **Company defaults on loan**  
    *Example*: If **Prateek**, **Krishna**, and **Kanishth‚Äôs** company in Delhi fails to repay the bank loan, the floating charge over its cloth stock crystallizes and becomes fixed.  
4. **Condition in the agreement**  
    Sometimes the loan agreement itself says that on certain events, the floating charge will become fixed.  
    *Example*: If **Raghavendra** lends money to a company in Varanasi and the agreement states that if profits fall below a certain level, the floating charge will become fixed, then it crystallizes when that condition occurs.

---

**Simple Illustration**

Suppose a textile company in Delhi is run by **Prateek**, **Krishna**, and **Kanishth**. They borrow ‚Çπ50 lakh from a bank in Lucknow. The bank takes a floating charge over the company‚Äôs stock of cloth. The company continues to buy and sell cloth every day. Later, the company fails to repay the loan. At that moment, the floating charge crystallizes and becomes fixed on whatever cloth stock is present in the warehouse.

On the other hand, if **Raghavendra** lends money to a company in Varanasi and takes the company‚Äôs factory building as security, that is a fixed charge from the beginning. The company cannot sell the building without his consent.

---

**Conclusion**

A floating charge is a security over assets that keep changing, while a fixed charge is tied to a specific asset. Floating charge gives flexibility to the company but is weaker for the lender.  The doctrine of lifting the veil is an exception to corporate personality. Courts use it cautiously, only when justice demands.  Corporate personality is the backbone of company law, but it is not absolute. **3\. Do you agree that in modern corporate jurisprudence the doctrine of ultra vires has diluted? Discuss with relevant cases and provisions.**

The doctrine of *ultra vires* is a fundamental principle in company law. The term ‚Äúultra vires‚Äù means ‚Äúbeyond powers.‚Äù When a company is incorporated, its powers and objectives are defined in the Memorandum of Association (MOA). **Traditional importance of ultra vires doctrine**  
 In early company law, the doctrine was very strict. Courts held that any act beyond the MOA was void and could not be validated.  
 Case: *Ashbury Railway Carriage and Iron Co. Ltd. v. Riche* (1875) ‚Äì the company entered into a contract to finance railway construction, which was beyond its MOA. The House of Lords held the contract void as *ultra vires*.

This strict approach ensured that companies stuck to their stated objectives, protecting investors and creditors.

**Dilution in modern jurisprudence**  
 Over time, the doctrine has been diluted due to several reasons:

1. **Wide drafting of objects clause**  
    Companies now draft very broad objects clauses in their MOA. For example, if Raghavendra and Laukendra form a company in Varanasi, they may include ‚Äúto carry on any lawful business‚Äù in their MOA. This makes almost every activity intra vires (within powers).  
2. **Statutory reforms**  
    The Companies Act, 2013 in India has reduced the importance of the doctrine. Section 4 requires companies to state their objects, but courts and regulators allow wide interpretation. Moreover, acts beyond MOA are not automatically void if they are within the law.  
3. **Protection of third parties**  
    Modern jurisprudence protects outsiders dealing with the company. If Saurabh contracts with a company in Kanpur, he should not suffer just because the company acted beyond its MOA. Courts often uphold such contracts to protect innocent third parties.  
4. **Shift towards directors‚Äô accountability**  
    Instead of declaring acts void, modern law holds directors accountable for misuse of powers. For example, if Satyam and Sachkeerat misuse company funds for activities not in MOA, shareholders can sue them for breach of duty, but the contract itself may still bind the company.  
5. **Case law showing dilution**

   * *Bell v. Lever Brothers Ltd.* (1932) ‚Äì courts moved towards protecting contracts unless there was clear illegality.  
   * In India, courts have emphasized that ultra vires acts harming shareholders can be challenged, but outsiders should not be penalized.

**Practical example for clarity**  
 Suppose Prateek, Krishna, and Kanishth form ‚ÄúLucknow Textiles Ltd.‚Äù with the object of manufacturing cloth. Later, the company invests in a hotel business in Allahabad. Traditionally, this would be void as *ultra vires*. But under modern law, the act may still be valid if shareholders approve and if it does not harm creditors. The directors may be questioned, but the hotel contract itself may not be struck down.

**Critical analysis**  
 The dilution of ultra vires doctrine has both advantages and disadvantages.

* **Advantages:** It gives companies flexibility to diversify and grow. It protects outsiders who deal with the company in good faith.  
* **Disadvantages:** It reduces the strict control over directors and may allow misuse of corporate funds.

Thus, while the doctrine still exists, its rigid application has been softened. Courts now focus more on protecting stakeholders rather than mechanically invalidating acts.

**Conclusion**  
 Yes, in modern corporate jurisprudence, the doctrine of ultra vires has been diluted. Earlier, any act beyond MOA was void, but now companies draft broad objects, statutes protect third parties, and directors are held accountable instead of invalidating contracts. This ensures flexibility for businesses like those formed by Prateek, Krishna, and Kanishth, while still safeguarding shareholders and creditors. The doctrine has not disappeared, but its strictness has been reduced to balance corporate growth with accountability.

**10\. What is related party transaction? Discuss with relevant provisions and cases.**

A related party transaction (RPT) means a transaction between a company and persons or entities that are closely connected to it. These include directors, key managerial personnel, relatives of directors, or other companies in which directors have significant interest. The idea is that such transactions may not always be at arm‚Äôs length and could be used to benefit insiders at the cost of shareholders. Therefore, the Companies Act, 2013 lays down strict rules to regulate related party transactions.

**Meaning of related party**  
 Section 2(76) of the Companies Act, 2013 defines ‚Äúrelated party.‚Äù It includes:

* Directors and their relatives.  
* Key managerial personnel and their relatives.  
* Firms or private companies in which directors are partners or members.  
* Public companies where directors hold significant shares.  
* Subsidiary, holding, or associate companies.

For example, if Prateek is a director of ‚ÄúLucknow Traders Ltd.‚Äù and his brother Krishna owns a firm in Varanasi, any contract between Lucknow Traders Ltd. and Krishna‚Äôs firm will be a related party transaction.

**Examples of related party transactions**

1. Sale or purchase of goods.  
2. Leasing of property.  
3. Providing or receiving services.  
4. Transfer of resources or obligations.  
5. Appointment of relatives to office or place of profit.

**Provisions under Companies Act, 2013 (Section 188\)**

1. **Board approval** ‚Äì Related party transactions must be approved by the Board of Directors.  
2. **Shareholders‚Äô approval** ‚Äì If transactions exceed prescribed limits (like 10% of turnover or net worth), approval of shareholders by special resolution is required.  
3. **Disclosure** ‚Äì Directors must disclose their interest in related party transactions.  
4. **Arm‚Äôs length principle** ‚Äì Transactions must be at arm‚Äôs length, meaning they should be conducted as if between unrelated parties.  
5. **Restriction on voting** ‚Äì Related parties cannot vote on resolutions approving related party transactions.

**Case laws on related party transactions**

1. *Tata Consultancy Services v. Cyrus Mistry* (2017) ‚Äì highlighted concerns about related party dealings and corporate governance.  
2. *GlaxoSmithKline Pharmaceuticals Ltd.* case ‚Äì SEBI emphasized disclosure of related party transactions to protect minority shareholders.  
3. *LIC v. Escorts Ltd.* (1986) ‚Äì though not directly about RPT, it emphasized transparency in dealings involving connected parties.

**Practical example for clarity**  
 Suppose ‚ÄúKanpur Developers Ltd.‚Äù has directors Prateek and Krishna. Krishna owns a construction firm in Allahabad. The company decides to give a contract to Krishna‚Äôs firm. This is a related party transaction. To ensure fairness, the Board must approve it, Krishna must disclose his interest, and if the contract exceeds limits, shareholders like Kanishth, Raghavendra, Laukendra, Saurabh, Satyam, and Sachkeerat must approve it by special resolution. This prevents misuse of company funds and protects minority shareholders.

**Critical analysis**  
 Related party transactions are not always bad. Sometimes they are necessary, especially in group companies or family businesses. For example, subsidiaries often transact with holding companies. However, the risk is that insiders may misuse their position to benefit themselves. Therefore, law requires disclosure, approval, and fairness. Independent directors also play a role in monitoring such transactions.

**Conclusion**  
 Related party transactions are dealings between a company and persons closely connected to it, such as directors, relatives, or associated companies. Section 188 of the Companies Act, 2013 regulates these transactions by requiring Board and shareholder approval, disclosure, and arm‚Äôs length principle. Cases like *Tata Consultancy Services v. Cyrus Mistry* highlight the importance of transparency. For companies formed by Prateek, Krishna, Kanishth, Raghavendra, Laukendra, Saurabh, Satyam, and Sachkeerat, related party transactions must be carefully monitored to ensure fairness and protect minority shareholders. Thus, regulation of RPTs is essential for good corporate governance.
