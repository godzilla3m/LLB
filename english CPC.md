### Definitions

| **Order** | Section 2(14) | A decision of the court which is not a decree. | During the case, the court directs Krishna to submit documents. This direction is an **order**. |

| **Mesne Profits** | Section 2(12) | Money earned by a person who wrongly possesses anotherâ€™s property. | Kanishth illegally lives in Prateekâ€™s house in Jaipur and earns rent from it. The rent earned is **mesne profits**. |

### Important Concepts 

| Concept | Section (CPC) | Simple Meaning | Easy Example |
| ----- | ----- | ----- | ----- |
| **Res Sub Judice** (stay of Suit) | Section 10 | A court will not try a case if the same matter is already pending in another court. | Prateek files a property case against Krishna in a Bhopal court. While it is still pending, Krishna files the same case in Indore. The Indore court will stop the case due to **res sub judice**. |
| **Res Judicata** | Section 11 | A matter once finally decided by a court cannot be filed again between the same parties. | A Delhi court decides a land dispute between Kanishth and Raghavendra. Later, Kanishth files the same case again in Jaipur. The court will reject it due to **res judicata**. |
| **Bar to Further Suit** | Section 12 | When a plaintiff is barred from filing another suit on the same cause of action. | Satyam sues Laukendra for shop rent in Delhi. After losing, he cannot file another suit for the same rent again. This is a **bar to further suit**. |
| **Caveat** | Section 148A | A notice given to the court to hear a person before passing any order. **In other words**, a person requests the court to hear them before passing any order. | Sachkeerat expects Prateek to file a case against him in Mumbai. Sachkeerat files a **caveat** so the court hears him before passing any order. |
| **Inherent Powers of Court** | Section 151 | Court has power to act for justice even if it is not mentioned in the CPC. | There is no clear rule for a situation in a case between Laukendra and Krishna in Pune. The court uses its **inherent powers** to pass a fair order. |

**Res Sub Judice VS Res Judicata (CPC)**

| Point of Comparison | Res Sub Judice | Res Judicata |
| ----- | ----- | ----- |
| **Section** | Section 10 CPC | Section 11 CPC |
| **Stage of Case** | When a case is **pending** | After a case is **finally decided** |
| **Meaning** | Stops trial of a suit already pending in another court | Bars filing of a fresh suit on the same matter |
| **Purpose** | To avoid parallel proceedings | To give finality to court decisions |
| **Effect** | Trial of later suit is stayed | Fresh suit is completely barred |
| **Condition** | Same matter, same parties, same issue | Same matter, same parties, same issue decided earlier |
| **Nature** | Temporary bar | Permanent bar |
| **Public Policy** | Prevents conflicting judgments | Prevents multiplicity of litigation |

## Q1. "Affidavit is the integral part of the institution of suits." Elaborate provisions related to affidavit and institution of suit said in the above statement.

The **institution of a suit** is the formal act of commencing a civil legal proceeding in a competent court of law.

An **Affidavit** is a formal, voluntary, written statement of facts made by a person, known as the **deponent** or **affiant**, who swears or affirms that the contents are true.

**Affidavit as an Integral Part of Institution of Suits**

Civil Procedure Code, 1908 (CPC) lays down the rules for filing and conducting civil cases in India. One important requirement in the institution of suits is the use of **affidavits**. An affidavit is a written statement made under oath. It is signed by the person making it and verified before an authorized officer. The purpose of an affidavit is to place facts before the court in a reliable manner.

The statement â€œAffidavit is the integral part of the institution of suitsâ€ means that affidavits play a central role in starting and supporting a civil case. Without affidavits, the court may not have proper evidence to proceed at the initial stage.

**Provisions Related to Institution of Suit**

1. **Section 26 CPC** â€“ A suit is instituted by presenting a plaint to the court.  
   * A plaint is the written document in which the plaintiff states his claim.  
   * Example: *Krishna* files a plaint in Allahabad court claiming that *Prateek* has illegally occupied his land in Lucknow.  
2. **Order IV CPC (Institution of Suits)** â€“ Explains how a suit begins. The plaint must be filed, and the court registers the case.  
   * Example: *Satyam* files a plaint in Varanasi court against *Laukendra* for recovery of money. The court registers the suit after checking the plaint.  
3. **Order VI CPC (Pleadings Generally)** â€“ Pleadings must contain material facts. They should be clear and not vague.  
   * Example: *Sachkeerat* files a plaint stating that *Raghavendra* borrowed â‚¹50,000 and did not return. He must mention dates and facts clearly.

Thus, institution of suit means filing a plaint with proper pleadings, supported by documents and affidavits.

>**ðŸ’¡ Tip:** **Pleadings** refers to the formal written statements filed in court by the partiesâ€”the **Plaint** by the plaintiff and the **Written Statement** by the defendant.

**Order VI, Rule 2(1)** explicitly states: "Every pleading shall contain, and contain only, a statement in a concise form of the **material facts** on which the party pleading relies for his claim or defence, as the case may be, but not the evidence by which they are to be proved."

**What are Material Facts?**

* **Material Facts** are the primary, essential, and basic facts that must be proved by a party to establish their **cause of action** (for the plaintiff) or their **defence** (for the defendant).  
* They are the facts that give the party the **right to the relief** they seek.  
* The legal term for material facts is ***Facta Probanda*** (facts that need to be proved).

**Example:** If a plaintiff, **A**, files a suit against a defendant, **B**, for a **breach of contract**, the **material facts** that **A** must plead would include:

1. Existence of a valid contract between **A** and **B** (e.g., date and place the contract was signed).  
2. The specific terms of the contract.  
3. The specific act or omission by **B** that constituted the breach.  
4. The fact that **A** suffered a loss or injury due to the breach.

If **A** omits a single material fact, like the existence of the contract itself, the pleading may be considered defective, leading to the suit's dismissal as an **incomplete cause of action**.

**Material Facts vs. Evidence**

The rule strictly mandates that pleadings must contain **material facts** but **not the evidence** by which they are to be proved.

* **Evidence** is the means by which the material facts are proved.  
* The legal term for evidence is ***Facta Probantia*** (facts proving the facts).

**Example:** In the contract breach suit:

* **Material Fact (Facta Probanda):** The defendant **B** failed to deliver the 100 bags of cement on the agreed date of 1st July 2025\.  
* **Evidence (Facta Probantia) \- NOT to be included in the Plaint:** The email correspondence sent on 2nd July 2025 where **A** asked **B** about the non-delivery, and the testimony of the delivery truck driver who witnessed the non-delivery.

The *pleading* must only state the failure to deliver (the material fact); the *trial* is where the email and the witness testimony (the evidence) are presented to prove that fact.

The **Particulars of a Plaint** are the specific, mandatory details that a plaintiff must include in the document, as primarily governed by **Order VII, Rule 1** of the Code of Civil Procedure (CPC).

These particulars ensure the court has all necessary information to proceed and include:

1. **Court and Parties:** The **name of the Court** where the suit is brought, and the **name, description, and place of residence** of both the plaintiff and the defendant.  
2. **Cause of Action:** The **facts** that constitute the **cause of action** (the legal reason for the suit) and **when** that cause of action arose.  
3. **Jurisdiction:** The facts showing that the Court has the **territorial and pecuniary jurisdiction** to hear the matter.  
4. **Relief:** The **specific relief** (remedy) that the plaintiff claims (e.g., money recovery, injunction, possession).  
5. **Valuation:** A statement of the **value of the subject-matter** of the suit for the purposes of court fees and jurisdiction.  
6. **Disability (if applicable):** A statement if the plaintiff or defendant is a **minor** or a person of **unsound mind**.  
7. **Relinquishment (if applicable):** Any amount the plaintiff has **relinquished** or allowed as a set-off.

Failing to include these material particulars may lead to the rejection of the plaint.

## Q2. "Stay of suit is incorporated in the Code with the view to stop the pendency of multiple suits in the same cause of action and the provision of bar to further suit works similar pattern also." Explain the related provisions and refer to the leading cases.

**Introduction**  
 Stay of suit and bar to further suit are important provisions in the Civil Procedure Code, 1908\. They are designed to prevent multiple suits on the same cause of action. The aim is to avoid duplication, waste of time, and conflicting judgments. These provisions protect both the parties and the court system by ensuring that one dispute is decided once and for all.

**Stay of Suit â€“ Section 10 CPC (Res Sub Judice)**  
 Section 10 CPC provides that when a matter is already pending in one court, another court cannot proceed with a suit on the same matter between the same parties. The second suit must be stayed until the first suit is decided. This ensures that two courts do not decide the same issue at the same time.  
 *Example*: Krishna files a suit in Allahabad court against Prateek for ownership of land in Lucknow. Later, Prateek files another suit in Varanasi court against Krishna for the same land. The Varanasi suit will be stayed until the Allahabad case is decided.

**Res Judicata â€“ Section 11 CPC**  
 Section 11 CPC says that once a matter has been finally decided between the same parties, it cannot be reopened. This is different from stay of suit because here the matter has already been decided.  
 *Example*: Krishna loses the land case in Allahabad. He cannot file another suit in Kanpur court for the same land against Prateek. The principle of res judicata bars him from doing so.

**Bar to Further Suit â€“ Section 12 CPC**  
 Section 12 CPC prevents repeated suits on the same cause of action. The purpose is to stop harassment of the defendant and to maintain finality of litigation.  
 *Example*: Satyam sues Laukendra in Varanasi for recovery of rent and loses. He cannot file another suit again for the same rent. The law bars him from filing further suits on the same issue.

**Example: Bar to Further Suit (Section 12 read with Order II, Rule 2\)**

Let us use the names **Prateek** (Plaintiff) and **Krishna** (Defendant), and a place, **Bhopal**, for the example.

**Scenario**

**Prateek** rented his warehouse in **Bhopal** to **Krishna** for two years, from 2023 to 2025\. By the time the lease expired, **Krishna** owed **Prateek** two things due to a single cause of action (breach of the rental agreement):

1. **Recovery of Possession:** The physical return of the warehouse.  
2. **Mesne Profits (Damages):** Compensation for the illegal use of the property for six months after the lease expired (valued at â‚¹1,00,000).

**The Procedural Default**

1. **First Suit (2025):** **Prateek** files a suit against **Krishna** in the Bhopal Civil Court.  
2. **Omission:** In the Plaint, Prateek consciously sues **only** for the **Recovery of Possession** of the warehouse. He deliberately **omits** the claim for the **Mesne Profits** (â‚¹1,00,000) without asking the court for permission to file a separate suit for the money later.  
3. **Result of First Suit:** The court hears the case and passes a decree ordering **Krishna** to hand over possession of the warehouse to **Prateek**.

**The Application of Section 12**

After winning the possession of the property, **Prateek** files a **Second Suit** against **Krishna** to claim the previously omitted **Mesne Profits** of â‚¹1,00,000.

* **The Bar:** The Bhopal Civil Court will now apply **Order II, Rule 2** (The Rule) along with **Section 12** (The Bar).  
* **Order II, Rule 2** states that since the claims for possession and mesne profits both arose from the **same cause of action** (the illegal retention of the warehouse by Krishna), and **Prateek** deliberately omitted the claim for mesne profits in the first suit **without obtaining the courtâ€™s permission (leave)**, he is barred from claiming it later.  
* **Section 12** simply confirms this procedural bar: Since **Prateek** is **precluded by the rules (Order II, Rule 2\)** from instituting a further suit for that specific monetary claim, he is **not entitled** to file the second suit.

**Conclusion:** The court will **dismiss the second suit**. **Prateek** loses his right to claim the â‚¹1,00,000 forever because he failed to include his entire claim in the initial pleading or seek the court's permission to split the claims. Section 12 acts as a gatekeeper, ensuring litigants are diligent and do not harass opponents with fragmented, multiple lawsuits arising from the same core dispute.

**Leading Cases**

* *Satyadhyan Ghosal v. Deorajin Debi (AIR 1960 SC 941\)*: The Supreme Court explained res judicata. It said that once a matter is decided, it cannot be reopened even in the same proceedings. This case shows the importance of finality in litigation.  
* *National Institute of Mental Health and Neuro Sciences v. C. Parameshwara (2005)*: The Supreme Court explained Section 10 CPC. It said that the object of Section 10 is to prevent courts of concurrent jurisdiction from simultaneously trying two parallel suits. This avoids conflicting decisions.  
* *Forward Construction Co. v. Prabhat Mandal (AIR 1986 SC 391\)*: The Supreme Court explained that res judicata applies not only to issues actually decided but also to issues that could have been raised. This prevents parties from splitting their claims and filing multiple suits.

**Difference Between Stay of Suit and Bar to Further Suit**  
 Stay of suit applies when two suits are pending at the same time. Bar to further suit applies when one suit has already been decided and another is attempted. Both provisions work to prevent duplication and harassment.

The difference between **Bar to Further Suit** (primarily **Section 12 CPC**) and **Res Judicata** (**Section 11 CPC**) lies in the **basis of the bar**â€”whether it is due to a final decision on the *merits* or a final decision based on a *procedural default*.

>**Tip ðŸŽ‰**
Bar to further suit focuses on the **Cause of Action**â€”it prohibits splitting the same cause of action into multiple suits.
Res Judicata focuses on the **Issue** or **Matter**â€”it prohibits re-agitating an issue already adjudicated.

**Conclusion**  
 Stay of suit under Section 10 CPC, res judicata under Section 11 CPC, and bar to further suit under Section 12 CPC are essential provisions. They stop multiple suits on the same cause of action. They ensure finality of litigation and prevent conflicting judgments. Leading cases like *Satyadhyan Ghosal*, *National Institute of Mental Health and Neuro Sciences*, and *Forward Construction Co.* have explained these principles. 

>**In simple words**, once a matter is pending in one court, another court must wait, and once a matter is decided, it cannot be reopened. These rules save time, reduce harassment, and uphold justice.

## Q3. Discuss the Rules 1 to 7 of the parties to suits of Order-I of the Code of Civil Procedure.

Order I (1) of the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 deals with the parties to a suit. **Rules 1 to 7 explain who can be joined as plaintiffs or defendants** and how misjoinder or non-joinder of parties is treated. These rules aim to avoid multiple suits and ensure complete and effective adjudication of disputes.

**Rule 1 of Order I (1) explains who may be joined as plaintiffs.** All persons may be joined as plaintiffs in one suit if the right to relief arises out of the same act or transaction, or series of acts or transactions, and if common questions of law or fact arise. 

For example, Prateek, Kanishth, and Saurabh jointly purchase land in Jaipur. Krishna illegally occupies the land. All three owners can jointly file one suit as plaintiffs against Krishna because their right arises from the same transaction and common facts.

**Rule 2 of Order I (1) gives power to the court to order separate trials.** Even when several plaintiffs are joined, the court may order separate trials if it feels that joint trial may cause inconvenience or confusion. 

For example, Prateek and Raghavendra file a joint suit in Delhi against Krishna and Satyam, but their claims are slightly different. The court may order separate trials to ensure fairness.

**Rule 3 of Order I (1) deals with who may be joined as defendants.** All people can be made defendants in one case if:

1. The relief (remedy) asked against them comes from the same act or transaction, and  
2. The case involves common questions of law or fact for all of them.

**Example for clarity:**  

Suppose Prateek, Krishna, and Kanishth together sell defective goods in Lucknow. Raghavendra sues them for compensation. Since the claim arises from the same transaction (sale of goods) and the legal question (defective goods liability) is common, all three can be joined as defendants in one case.

**Rule 4 of Order I (1) clarifies that judgment may be given without amendment.** If the court finds that the relief is available against one or more defendants, it can pass a decree against them even if all defendants are not liable. 

ðŸ˜€ Example: 

**Scenario**

1. **Prateek** orders 50 custom-printed t-shirts from **Krishna's** printing shop in Pune.  
2. **Saurabh** is the person Krishna hired to deliver the shirts.

4. **Prateek** is uncertain whether the delay and mistake were due to **Krishna** (printing shop's fault) or **Saurabh** (delivery person's mistake/delay).  
5. To cover all bases (using Order I, Rule 7), **Prateek** files a single suit against **both Krishna and Saurabh** for the â‚¹20,000 loss.

**Application of Order I, Rule 4(1)**

1. **Trial Outcome:** During the trial, evidence is presented.  
   * The court finds that **Krishna** printed the shirts correctly and on time, but the delay was caused solely because **Saurabh** mishandled the delivery and delivered the wrong batch to the wrong place first.  
   * The court determines that **Krishna is NOT liable**, but **Saurabh IS liable** for the â‚¹20,000 loss.  
2. **Judgment:** The court, applying **Order I, Rule 4(1)**, does the following:  
   * It **dismisses the suit** against **Krishna**.  
   * It passes a **decree** (judgment) ordering **Saurabh** to pay **Prateek** the â‚¹20,000 in damages.

**Conclusion:** The key point is that the court did **not** have to throw out the entire suit just because Krishna was wrongly included. The rule allows the court to efficiently deliver justice by passing judgment against the **one person found liable** (**Saurabh**), even though another person (**Krishna**) was included in the same initial suit.

**Rule 5 of Order I (1) states that it is not necessary that every defendant be interested in all reliefs.** A defendant can be concerned with only part of the relief claimed. In other words, In one suit, there may be several defendants and several reliefs. It is not necessary that every defendant must be connected to all the reliefs. A defendant can be concerned with only some of them.

**Example-1**, in a suit filed by Laukendra in Kanpur, Krishna may be liable for money recovery, while Saurabh may be liable only for possession. Both can still be defendants in the same suit.

* One player breaks all three rules,  
* Another breaks two rules,

The third breaks only one rule.

* Even though their faults are different, they are all part of the same match â€” so they can be dealt with together.

**Rule 6 of Order I (1) allows the plaintiff to join alternative defendants.** When there is doubt as to who is liable, the plaintiff may join two or more defendants in the alternative. 

For example, Prateek is unsure whether Krishna or Raghavendra received his payment in a business deal in Mumbai. He can sue both in the alternative, and the court will decide who is actually liable.

**Meaning of â€œalternativeâ€ in this context:** â€œAlternativeâ€ means *either one or the other, but not both together*. When the plaintiff is not sure which defendant is actually responsible, he can include both as defendants in the same suit. The court will then decide which one is liable.

>ðŸ‘‰ So, â€œalternativeâ€ here simply means **when there is doubt, you can sue multiple people, and the court will choose the correct one who is responsible.**

**Rules 1 to 7 of Order I (1) CPC provide a flexible and practical framework for joinder of parties to a suit.** These rules help in avoiding multiplicity of litigation, saving time and cost, and ensuring complete justice. The principle of res judicata applies when:

* The matter directly and substantially in issue has been decided.  
* The earlier decision was by a competent court.  
* The parties in both suits are the same or claim under the same title.  
* The earlier decision was final.

**Restrictions on further suits:**  
 The CPC imposes restrictions so that parties cannot file endless suits.

**Six Major Differences between Decree and Order**

1. **Definition**

   * Order: Formal expression of any decision of a civil court which is not a decree.  
2. **Scope**

   * Decree arises only in a suit instituted by presentation of a plaint.  
   * Order may arise in a suit or in other proceedings (like applications).  
3. **Conclusive Nature**

   * Decree conclusively determines rights of parties.  
   * Order may or may not finally determine rights; often procedural.  
4. **Appealability**

   * Every decree is appealable unless expressly barred.  
   * Only certain orders are appealable under Section 104 and Order 43 CPC.  
5. **Number**

   * In one suit, there can be only one decree (though it may be preliminary or final).  
   * In one suit, there can be multiple orders at different stages.  
6. **Examples**

   * Decree: Court declares that Raghavendra is the rightful owner of land in Kanpur.  
   * Order: Court directs Laukendra to pay interim costs or produce evidence.

## Q7. Discuss the Triple Test principles for granting the injunction order under the code of civil procedure.

**Triple Test Principles for Granting Injunction Order under CPC**

**Meaning of Injunction**  

An injunction is a judicial order restraining a party from doing certain acts or directing them to perform certain acts. Under the Code of Civil Procedure (CPC), temporary injunctions are governed by **Order XXXIX Rules 1 and 2**. Courts grant injunctions to preserve rights and prevent irreparable harm until the final decision.

For example, if Prateek files a suit against Krishna to stop construction on disputed land in Lucknow, he may seek a temporary injunction to restrain Krishna until the case is decided.

**Triple Test Principles**  

Courts follow three essential tests before granting injunctions. These are known as the **Triple Test Principles**:

1. **Prima Facie Case**  
   * The applicant must show a genuine case with legal merit.  
   * It does not mean proving the case finally, but showing that there is a serious question to be tried.  
   * Example: Raghavendra claims ownership of land in Kanpur and produces sale deed. Court sees that there is a valid dispute. This establishes prima facie case.  
2. **Balance of Convenience**  
   * Court considers which party will suffer greater hardship if injunction is granted or refused.  
   * Example: Laukendra seeks injunction against Saurabh to stop demolition of his shop. If injunction is refused, Laukendra loses shop permanently. If granted, Saurabh only faces delay. Balance of convenience favors Laukendra.  
3. **Irreparable Injury**  
   * Applicant must show that refusal of injunction would cause harm which cannot be compensated by money.  
   * Example: Satyam seeks injunction against Sachkeerat to stop cutting of ancestral trees in Faizabad. If trees are cut, they cannot be restored. This is irreparable injury.

**Application of Triple Test**  

Courts apply all three tests together. If any one test fails, injunction may not be granted.

* Example: Kanishth seeks injunction against Prateek to stop publication of article. Court finds no prima facie case because article is not defamatory. Injunction is refused.

**Illustrative Example**  

Suppose Prateek sues Krishna claiming ownership of farmland in Lucknow. Krishna starts construction of a factory.

* Prima facie case: Prateek shows sale deed proving ownership.  
* Balance of convenience: If injunction is refused, factory will be built and land altered permanently. If granted, Krishna only faces delay.

Irreparable injury: Once factory is built, farmland cannot be restored.

* Court grants injunction restraining Krishna from construction until case is decided.

**Judicial Approach**  

Courts emphasize that injunction is an equitable remedy, not a matter of right. It is granted only when all three tests are satisfied. The principle ensures fairness and prevents misuse of injunctions to harass opponents.

**Conclusion**  

The Triple Testâ€”prima facie case, balance of convenience, and irreparable injuryâ€”is the guiding principle for granting injunctions under CPC. It ensures that injunctions are granted only in genuine cases where refusal would cause serious harm. This protects rights of parties while preventing abuse of process.

* To protect parties from harassment.  
* To uphold finality of judicial decisions.  
* To save judicial time.

**Essential Conditions for Application of Res Judicata**

1. **Matter Directly and Substantially in Issue**  
   * The matter in the subsequent suit must be directly and substantially the same as in the former suit.  
   * Example: Raghavendra sues Laukendra for possession of house in Kanpur. Later, Laukendra sues Raghavendra for same house. The issue is identical.  
2. **Same Parties or Parties Claiming Under Them**  
   * The parties in both suits must be the same or claiming under the same title.  
   * Example: Saurabh sues Satyam for recovery of loan. Later, Sachkeerat (legal heir of Satyam) sues Saurabh on same issue. Res Judicata applies.  
3. **Same Title in Both Suits**  
   * Parties must litigate under same title.  
   * Example: Krishna sues Prateek as landlord for rent. Later, Krishna sues Prateek as tenant for same rent. Different titlesâ€”Res Judicata does not apply.  
4. **Matter Heard and Finally Decided**  
   * The matter must have been heard and finally decided by the court.  
   * Example: If Kanishthâ€™s suit is dismissed for default, it is not Res Judicata because issue was not decided.  
5. **Court of Competent Jurisdiction**  
   * The former suit must have been decided by a court competent to try the subsequent suit.  
   * Example: If a small causes court decides a rent dispute, its decision binds parties in later suits.  
6. **Former Suit**  
   * The suit which was decided earlier is the former suit; the later one is the subsequent suit.

**Illustrative Example**  

Suppose Prateek sues Krishna in Lucknow claiming ownership of farmland. Court decides in favor of Prateek. Later, Krishna files another suit in Kanpur claiming same farmland.

* Issue is directly and substantially same.  
* Parties are same.  
* Title is same (ownership).  
* Matter was finally decided.

Court was competent.

* Thus, Res Judicata applies and second suit is barred.

**Case Laws**

* **Sheoparsan Singh v. Ramnandan Prasad (1916)**: Privy Council held that once matter is decided, it cannot be reopened.  
* **Satyadhyan Ghosal v. Deorajin Debi (1960)**: Supreme Court held Res Judicata applies even to interlocutory orders.  
* **Daryao v. State of U.P. (1961)**: Supreme Court held Res Judicata applies even to writ petitions.

**Conclusion**  

The doctrine of Res Judicata ensures finality of litigation and prevents harassment through repeated suits. Its essential conditions include same matter, same parties, same title, final decision, and competent court. By applying this doctrine, courts uphold justice and efficiency, ensuring that one dispute is decided once and for all.

   * Example: Sachkeeratâ€™s plaint should simply state â€œDefendant borrowed â‚¹50,000 on 1 Jan 2020 and failed to repay.â€

6. **Alternative Pleas Allowed**

7. **No Scandalous or Irrelevant Matter**  
   * Pleadings must not contain abusive or irrelevant statements.

8. **Amendment of Pleadings**

Pleadings are the foundation of civil litigation. They consist of plaint and written statement, governed by rules ensuring clarity, conciseness, and focus on material facts. By following these rules, courts can frame proper issues and deliver justice efficiently.

## Q2. What are the essential contents of a Decree? Whether the following orders are Decree? Explain with case laws:

**Essential contents of a Decree**  

A decree is defined under Section 2(2) of the Civil Procedure Code (CPC). It is the formal expression of an adjudication by a civil court which conclusively determines the rights of the parties with regard to all or any of the matters in controversy in the suit. The essentials are:

1. **Adjudication** â€“ There must be a judicial determination of rights.  
2. **By a Civil Court** â€“ It must be passed by a court having civil jurisdiction.  
3. **Determination of Rights** â€“ It must conclusively decide the rights of parties.  
4. **Matters in Controversy** â€“ It must relate to issues raised in the suit.  
5. **Formal Expression** â€“ It must be formally recorded.  
6. **Types** â€“ It may be preliminary, final, or partly preliminary and partly final.

An arbitral award is not a decree by itself. But once the court passes an order making the award enforceable under Section 36 of the Arbitration Act, it becomes executable as a decree.

* Case law: *Satish Kumar v. Surinder Kumar* (SC) held that an award becomes enforceable like a decree once it is made rule of court.  
* Example: Kanishth and Raghavendra submit their dispute to arbitration in Allahabad. Arbitrator gives award. Until court confirms it, it is not a decree. Once confirmed, it is treated as decree.

### (b) Final order passed in original petition

### (c) Compromise Decree  

When parties agree to settle and court passes a decree in terms of compromise, it is a decree. Section 2(2) CPC expressly includes compromise decrees.

* Case law: *Banwari Lal v. Chando Devi* (SC) held compromise decree is valid decree.  
* Example: Satyam and Sachkeerat compromise in a property dispute at Faizabad. Court records compromise. This is a decree.

**Conclusion**  

A decree must conclusively determine rights of parties in a civil suit. Arbitration award is not decree until made rule of court, final order in original petition is decree, and compromise decree is decree by definition.

## Q3. "One suit and one decision is enough for any single dispute." Is it a rule of convenience or absolute justice ? Explain.

**Introduction**  

The statement refers to the principle of *res judicata* under Section 11 of the Civil Procedure Code (CPC). It means that once a matter has been finally decided between the same parties by a competent court, it cannot be reopened in another suit. This principle ensures that there is finality in litigation. The question is whether this principle is only a rule of convenience or whether it is a rule of absolute justice.

**Meaning of Res Judicata**  

Res judicata literally means â€œa matter already judged.â€ It prevents parties from filing repeated suits on the same issue.

* Example: Prateek sues Krishna in Lucknow claiming ownership of a house. The court decides that Krishna is the owner. Later, Prateek cannot file another suit against Krishna on the same issue of ownership.  
* This saves time, prevents harassment, and ensures stability in legal rights.

**Rule of Convenience**  

At first glance, res judicata looks like a rule of convenience because it avoids multiple suits and saves judicial time. Courts cannot waste resources on the same dispute again and again.

* Example: If Kanishth sues Raghavendra for recovery of â‚¹50,000 loan in Allahabad, and the court decides, it would be inconvenient to allow Kanishth to file another suit for the same loan.  
* Thus, it is convenient for courts and parties.

**Rule of Absolute Justice**  

However, the deeper purpose of res judicata is not just convenience. It is based on public policy and justice. Justice requires that disputes must come to an end. Endless litigation would cause uncertainty and harassment.

* Case law: *Daryao v. State of UP* (SC) held that res judicata is a rule of public policy, not just convenience. It ensures finality of decisions and protects parties from repeated harassment.  
* Example: Laukendra sues Satyam for possession of land in Jaunpur. Court decides in favour of Satyam. If Laukendra is allowed to file another suit, Satyam would face endless harassment. Justice demands that one decision must be final.

**Balance of Convenience and Justice**  

Res judicata serves both purposes:

1. Convenience â€“ avoids multiplicity of suits.  
2. Justice â€“ ensures finality and protects parties.

But the stronger reason is justice. Convenience is secondary. The principle is absolute because it binds even higher courts. Once decided, even Supreme Court will not reopen the same issue between same parties.

**Exceptions**  

There are limited exceptions where res judicata does not apply:

* When the earlier decision was without jurisdiction.  
* When the matter was not directly in issue.

When circumstances have changed.

* Example: Sachkeerat sues Saurabh for trespass in Faizabad. Court dismisses suit because land was not proved. Later, Sachkeerat acquires ownership documents. He may file fresh suit because circumstances changed.

**Conclusion**  

The principle â€œone suit and one decision is enough for any single disputeâ€ is not merely a rule of convenience. It is a rule of absolute justice based on public policy. It ensures finality of litigation, prevents harassment, and upholds certainty in law. Convenience is only a byâ€‘product. Justice is the real foundation.
